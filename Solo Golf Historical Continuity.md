---
title: Solo Golf Historical Continuity
date: 2026-05-29
---

Golf began on the eastern coast of Scotland on stretches of open links land where the activity was, at its earliest, largely unregulated and unbounded. A player needed only a ball, a stick, and ground to cross, and so the game in its formative period was naturally solitary or only loosely social. A person might walk the dunes alone, driving a ball toward a distant target and following it, or fall in casually with whoever happened to be on the same ground. There were no fixed numbers, no required partners, and no expectation that play should be communal. The links were common terrain, and golf moved across them in whatever form circumstance allowed, which often meant a single figure working out the line of a hole without company.

This openness narrowed as the game became organized. From the middle of the eighteenth century, formal clubs began to appear, beginning with bodies such as the gentlemen golfers of Edinburgh and the society that would become the Royal and Ancient at St Andrews. Clubs codified rules, set boundaries, and most consequentially established golf as a structured social occasion. Membership implied fellow members, and play was increasingly arranged among them. Through the nineteenth century the foursome and the grouped round became the recognized shape of the game, partly because matches between paired sides suited wagering and competition, and partly because the round acquired a social function: golf was something done in company, with the walk between shots serving conversation as much as sport. Solo play did not vanish, but it ceased to be the default. The expected unit of golf became two, three, or four players moving together, and the solitary golfer became the exception rather than the ordinary case.

Yet solo play persisted within the amateur game, and it did so consistently in two forms that have remained recognizable ever since. The first is deliberate practice. A golfer seeking to improve has always benefited from working alone, because repetition, experimentation, and concentration are easier without the rhythm and obligations of a group. Alone, a player can hit the same shot many times, try variations of grip or stance, play a second or third ball to test a different line, and pause to study a result without holding anyone up. The solitary practice round, whether on a quiet hole or across a full course, became the natural setting for skill development, and this association between solo golf and self-improvement has been stable across the game's history. The second form is circumstantial. A golfer playing at an off-peak hour, on a lightly used course, or simply preferring to walk at an unhurried pace often ends up alone not by design but by situation. Early mornings, weekday afternoons, quiet provincial links, and uncrowded municipal grounds have always produced solo rounds simply because no one else was present to join. In both forms the continuity is more striking than any disruption: the solitary golfer of the modern era practices and plays in much the same way and for much the same reasons as the solitary golfer of a century or more ago.

The modern amateur situation reflects this long continuity rather than any departure from it. Solo golf remains a limited but durable mode of play, neither dominant nor disappearing. It is most often chosen for focused improvement, where a player wants to work on the game without the constraints of a group, or for the value of personal time, where the round itself is the point and solitude is part of its appeal. Group play remains the default structure of amateur golf, the form most courses are organized around and most players expect, but the solitary round endures alongside it as a steady and accepted alternative, occupying the same narrow and consistent place it has held since the game first moved beyond the open links.
