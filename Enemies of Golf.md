title: Enemies of Golf
date: 2026-02-27

## Professional Golf

The tour turned a game into a spectacle and a career. Once the standard became what the best players in the world could do on perfect courses, everyone else inherited expectations they were never meant to carry: pristine conditions, scoring that means nothing to a weekend round, and an idea of "real golf" that has little to do with walking nine holes after work. Professionalism set the bar, and the bar has been crushing ordinary players ever since.

## Golf Carts

The cart severed golf from the walk. A round was once a few hours on foot through interesting ground; the cart reduced it to a series of drives between shots, hugging a path, racing a clock. It reshaped course design toward sprawl that no walker would tolerate, padded the cost of every round, and quietly removed the exercise and the pace that made the game worth a whole afternoon.

## Television Golf

Broadcast golf taught everyone to want the wrong things. Viewers absorbed tournament-green speeds, manicured fairways, and the hush of a final-round telecast as the definition of how the game should look and feel—conditions that bankrupt municipal courses trying to imitate them. It also flattened golf into something watched rather than played, a Sunday-afternoon background hum instead of a thing you do.

## Residential Golf Courses

When the course became an amenity to sell houses, the golf stopped mattering. These layouts answer to lot lines and frontage, not to good holes; out-of-bounds stakes guard backyards, and the routing serves the developer's plat. The result is real estate with a flag in it—courses designed to be looked at from a deck rather than played with any joy.

## Bluetooth Speakers

The speaker drags the noise of everywhere else onto the one ground that offered quiet. Golf's pace and silence are the point—the walk, the wind, the conversation or the absence of it. A playlist blaring from the next cart erases all of that for everyone within earshot, turning a shared, contemplative space into someone else's party.
