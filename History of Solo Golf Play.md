---
title: History of Solo Golf Play
date: 2026-05-29
---

In the early days of golf on the windswept links of Scotland, the game was often a solitary or loosely social affair. Players would set out alone or in small, ad hoc groups across the shared coastal terrain, striking balls towards natural targets with whatever clubs they carried. Formal organisation was minimal; the rhythm of play was dictated by personal whim, weather and the light.

The rise of golf clubs in the 18th and 19th centuries changed this. Institutions such as the Honourable Company of Edinburgh Golfers and the Royal and Ancient Golf Club at St Andrews introduced rules, competitions and regular fixtures. Social foursomes and matches between members became the norm, turning golf into a more structured communal pastime. Solo play did not vanish but was relegated to the margins of club life, pursued mainly by those who ventured out alone when the links were quiet.

Thereafter, amateur golf sustained two enduring forms of solitary play. The first was deliberate practice: solitary hours spent repeating swings, experimenting with technique and honing skills away from the scrutiny of others. The second was circumstantial: rounds fitted into off-peak hours or taken as brisk walks on lightly trafficked courses when companions were unavailable. In both cases, solo golf represented continuity rather than disruption—a quiet thread running through the game's social evolution.

Today the pattern holds. Group play remains the default for most amateurs, reflecting the game's club-based, sociable character. Solo golf persists as a limited but stable option, favoured by those seeking focused improvement or the simple pleasure of an unaccompanied round. It is neither marginalised nor dominant, but a practical choice for those who value time alone on the links.
