---
title: Solo Golf in Amateur History
date: 2026-05-29
---

Solo golf has existed for as long as the amateur game itself. In its earliest form in Scotland, golf was not structured around scheduled groups or formal tee times. People simply went to the links when they could. Playing alone was ordinary, especially on open coastal ground where the game was informal and fluid. A round might involve a single player working through shots, occasionally joined or observed by others, but without any strong expectation of group play.

As golf became organized through clubs in the 18th and 19th centuries, the amateur game shifted toward social structure. Clubs formalized etiquette, match play formats, and eventually foursomes as the standard unit of play. Golf increasingly became a social activity as much as a personal one. Within this framework, solo play was no longer the default expression of the game, even though it remained entirely permissible.

Despite this shift, solitary play never disappeared from the amateur tradition. It survived in two consistent forms. First, as practice: amateurs have long used solo rounds to work on swing changes, shot selection, and repetition without the social pacing of a group. Second, as circumstance: early or late tee times, low-traffic days, and rural or public courses often made solo play possible even when not formally encouraged.

By the 20th century, especially in the United States, public course growth made golf more accessible but also more regulated in flow. Pace-of-play management and revenue considerations meant that solo golfers were often paired with others, reducing the frequency of true solitary rounds during peak hours. Still, solo play persisted at the margins of the amateur game, especially among committed players who valued walking and uninterrupted concentration.

In the modern amateur game, solo golf occupies a quiet but stable niche. It is no longer the assumed form of play, but it remains a recognizable mode: used for focused practice, personal time, and off-peak rounds. Its role has narrowed, not disappeared. The amateur game today treats golf as primarily social in structure but still allows for a parallel tradition of solitary engagement that has never fully left the sport.
