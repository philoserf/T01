---
title: Hickory Era American Golf
date: 2026-05-24
---

The hickory era in American golf runs from the late 1880s, when the game was reintroduced after its early-19th-century collapse, to roughly 1935, when steel shafts had finally displaced hickory in tournament play. It opens almost as an accident. In February 1888 a Scottish-born linen merchant named John Reid laid out three holes in a cow pasture across from his house in Yonkers, played with clubs brought back by his friend Robert Lockhart from Old Tom Morris's shop in St Andrews, and within months had organized himself, Lockhart, Henry Tallmadge, and four others into the St Andrew's Golf Club. They moved twice—to an apple orchard (giving them the "Apple Tree Gang" nickname), then to Grey Oaks—and were soon joined by Shinnecock Hills on Long Island in 1891, the first incorporated American club with a Stanford White clubhouse; The Country Club at Brookline adopting golf in 1893; Newport Country Club the same year; and the Chicago Golf Club founded by Charles Blair Macdonald in 1893, the first eighteen-hole course in the United States. On 22 December 1894 these five clubs formed what became the United States Golf Association ￼, partly to settle a quarrel over which 1894 amateur championship had been the "real" national title.

The first generation of American competitive golf was almost entirely Scottish. The professionals at the early clubs—Willie Dunn, Willie Davis, Willie Anderson, Alex and Macdonald Smith, Fred and Tom McLeod, Jock Hutchison—were imports who built the courses, made the clubs in the back shop, gave the lessons, and won the championships. Willie Anderson took four U.S. Opens between 1901 and 1905. The amateur side, by contrast, was the preserve of wealthy clubmen: Macdonald won the first U.S. Amateur in 1895; H. J. Whigham, Findlay Douglas, Walter Travis (the first non-Briton to win the British Amateur, in 1904), and Jerome Travers carried the flag through the 1900s. Macdonald also imported something else: course architecture. His National Golf Links of America on Long Island, opened in 1911, was the first American course designed as deliberate homage to the great holes of Scotland and England, and it inaugurated the Golden Age of American course design.

The hinge of the era is the 1913 U.S. Open at The Country Club. The USGA had rescheduled the championship from June to September so that Harry Vardon, the five-time Open champion, and Ted Ray, the reigning Open champion, could play it on their American exhibition tour ￼; the two were expected to win at will. Francis Ouimet, a twenty-year-old former caddie who lived across the street from the course and worked at Wright & Ditson's sporting goods store, tied them over 72 holes and then, with a ten-year-old caddie named Eddie Lowery on his bag, beat them by five and six strokes respectively in the Saturday playoff. That victory gave golf an impetus in the United States that has accelerated ever since ￼. The game stopped being Scottish, Anglo-Episcopalian, and rich-only almost overnight; American participation roughly doubled within a decade, and a generation of working-class players—Walter Hagen out of a caddie yard in Rochester, Gene Sarazen out of an Italian immigrant family in Harrison, New York—found a way in.

The 1920s were the boom. Hagen won eleven majors between 1914 and 1929, dragged professional golfers into the front door of clubhouses they had previously been forbidden to enter, and effectively invented the modern golf professional as a personality. Sarazen took the U.S. Open and PGA in 1922 at age twenty. Bobby Jones, the Atlanta lawyer-amateur, won thirteen majors in eight years and capped the era with the 1930 Grand Slam—U.S. and British Amateurs, U.S. and British Opens—then retired at twenty-eight and a year later started building Augusta National with Alister MacKenzie. The PGA of America was founded in 1916, the Ryder Cup began in 1927, and the Golden Age architects—Donald Ross, A. W. Tillinghast, Seth Raynor, George Thomas, William Flynn, Hugh Wilson, Stanley Thompson across the border in Canada—laid out most of the courses that still anchor American major championship rota: Pinehurst No. 2, Oakmont, Merion, Pine Valley, Winged Foot, Baltusrol's Lower, Pebble Beach, Cypress Point, Riviera, Olympic, Bethpage Black.

Equipment defined what playing actually felt like. Shafts were second-growth American hickory, prized for its combination of stiffness and torsional give; the best came from a few specialized makers in Tennessee and Memphis. Heads were persimmon for the woods, forged carbon steel for the irons, and a player carried a personally curated set with names rather than numbers—driver, brassie, spoon, baffy, cleek, mid-iron, mashie, mashie-niblick, niblick, jigger, putter—typically eight to twelve clubs until the USGA capped the bag at fourteen in 1938. The transformative invention of the era was not the shaft but the ball: Coburn Haskell's rubber-cored ball, patented in 1898 and refined by the early 1900s, replaced the gutta-percha gutty, added thirty or more yards off the tee, made the game far more forgiving for amateurs, and forced courses to lengthen. Steel shafts were legalized by the USGA in 1924 and by the R&A in 1929; by 1935 hickory was largely gone from competitive play, and with it the era closed. America, which in 1880 had effectively no golf at all, finished the hickory period with more courses, more players, and more of the world's best players than any other country—a position it has not relinquished since.
